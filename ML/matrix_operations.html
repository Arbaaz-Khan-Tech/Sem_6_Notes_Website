Understanding Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) and Matrix Diagonalization

Core Concepts (V.V.IMP)

Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)

The Swiss Army Knife of Matrix Factorization — breaks any matrix into interpretable parts.

\[A = U\Sigma V^T\]

U: Left singular vectors (orthonormal basis)

Σ: Diagonal matrix (singular values = scaling factors)

V^T: Right singular vectors (orthonormal basis)

Example: SVD of a 2×2 Matrix

Let's decompose the matrix:

\[A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}\]

Step 1: Find A^T A

\[A^T A = \begin{bmatrix} 10 & 6 \\ 6 & 10 \end{bmatrix}\]

Step 2: Find Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

Eigenvalues: λ₁ = 16, λ₂ = 4

Eigenvectors: v₁ = [1/√2, 1/√2], v₂ = [-1/√2, 1/√2]

Step 3: Construct V and Σ

\[V = \begin{bmatrix} 1/√2 & -1/√2 \\ 1/√2 & 1/√2 \end{bmatrix}\]

\[\Sigma = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}\]

Step 4: Find U

\[U = AV\Sigma^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} 1/√2 & -1/√2 \\ 1/√2 & 1/√2 \end{bmatrix}\]

Shortcut to Remember:

"SVD = UV's Secret! U = Input Space, Σ = Stretch, V^T = Output Space"

Matrix Diagonalization

Turns matrices into their simplest (diagonal) form for easy analysis.

\[A = PDP^{-1}\]

P: Matrix of eigenvectors

D: Diagonal matrix of eigenvalues

P^-1: Inverse of eigenvector matrix

Example: Diagonalization of a 2×2 Matrix

Let's diagonalize the matrix:

\[A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 1 & 4 \end{bmatrix}\]

Step 1: Find Eigenvalues

Characteristic equation: det(A - λI) = 0

\[(4-λ)^2 - 1 = 0\]

Eigenvalues: λ₁ = 5, λ₂ = 3

Step 2: Find Eigenvectors

For λ₁ = 5: v₁ = [1, 1]

For λ₂ = 3: v₂ = [-1, 1]

Step 3: Construct P and D

\[P = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\]

\[D = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix}\]

Step 4: Verify A = PDP⁻¹

\[P^{-1} = \frac{1}{2}\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\]

\[PDP^{-1} = A\]

Shortcut to Remember:

"Diagonalization = PDP Party! P = People, D = Dance Floor"

Detailed Explanation

SVD in Detail

Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) is a powerful matrix factorization technique that works for any matrix, whether square or rectangular. Here's why it's important:

  • Universal Applicability: Works for any matrix shape
  • Numerical Stability: More stable than eigendecomposition
  • Dimensionality Reduction: Used in PCA and image compression

Key Properties:

  1. U contains the left singular vectors, representing the input space
  2. Σ contains the singular values, showing the importance of each dimension
  3. V^T contains the right singular vectors, representing the output space

Matrix Diagonalization in Detail

Matrix Diagonalization is a special case of matrix decomposition that works only for square matrices. Here's what makes it special:

  • Square Matrices Only: Requires the matrix to be square
  • Eigenvalue Decomposition: Based on eigenvalues and eigenvectors
  • Power Calculations: Makes computing matrix powers easier

Key Properties:

  1. P contains the eigenvectors as columns
  2. D is a diagonal matrix of eigenvalues
  3. P⁻¹ is the inverse of the eigenvector matrix
